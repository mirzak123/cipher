vigenere_crack.py — How it works
=================================

STEP 1 — Find the key length (Index of Coincidence)

For each candidate key length 1–20, deal the ciphertext into that many
groups (round-robin). If the guess is correct, each group is a simple
Caesar cipher and its letter distribution looks like English (IoC ≈ 0.065).
Compute the average IoC across groups. The key length with the highest
average IoC is the best candidate. In our case: key length 14.

STEP 2 — Kasiski Examination

Scan the ciphertext for repeated 3/4/5-letter sequences. Compute the
distance between each pair of occurrences. These distances must be
multiples of the key length. Take the GCD of all distances as a
candidate. (In practice the GCD often degrades to 1, but the factor
frequency from Step 1 already pointed us to 14.)

STEP 3 — Recover the key and decrypt (Chi-squared)

For each key length 1–20: split ciphertext into groups, then for each
group try all 26 Caesar shifts. Pick the shift whose decrypted letter
frequencies best match English (lowest chi-squared score). That shift
is one letter of the key. Decrypt the full ciphertext with the
recovered key and print it. The human picks the most readable output.
Key length 14 produces the most recognizable English.
